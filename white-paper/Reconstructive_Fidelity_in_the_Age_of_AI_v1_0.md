# Reconstructive Fidelity in the Age of AI

*The Invariant Distance Principle and Why Governance Requires Evidence That Survives Institutional Change*

*For legal, risk, audit, compliance, and AI-governance leaders responsible for reconstructing consequential AI-assisted activity over time.*

**Ryan Feller**  
Fork / NGAST  
Version 1.0 — June 11, 2026

AI systems are increasingly involved in consequential institutional work. They draft, classify, summarize, route, recommend, and support decisions in areas such as credit, employment, healthcare, supplier risk, security, legal review, and internal controls.

They also change—frequently.

Models are updated. Prompts and retrieval sources are revised. Product teams restructure workflows. Policies change. Staff move on. Vendors are replaced. Authority shifts among legal, risk, engineering, compliance, audit, and operations.

Months or years later, institutions still confront familiar questions:

- Why was this application denied?
- Why was this claim escalated?
- Why was this vendor approved?
- What did the AI receive and produce?
- What did a human reviewer actually see, alter, or accept?
- Which policy, model, and workflow configuration were operative at the time?
- What remained uncertain or unchecked?

The technical environment in which these questions arise is different from the one in which many institutional evidence practices were designed. AI-assisted activity is expanding and changing faster than many organizations’ existing documentation, review, and evidence-preservation practices were built to accommodate.

This creates a specific governance problem: not only controlling what an AI system may do today, but preserving enough structured evidence that a future reviewer can reconstruct what occurred without relying solely on what the institution later says about it.

That is the problem of reconstructive fidelity.

---

## Governance Already Demands Traceability

Traceability is no longer peripheral to AI governance.

NIST’s voluntary Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework organizes AI risk management around four Core functions—GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE. The Framework emphasizes documented accountability structures, clearly assigned human roles and responsibilities, ongoing monitoring, periodic review, incident response, recovery, and change management. [1]

The European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act imposes more specific requirements for high-risk AI systems. Article 12 requires those systems to technically support automatic event logging over their lifetime. Article 19(1) requires providers to retain automatically generated logs under their control for an appropriate period of at least six months, subject to applicable Union or national law. Article 26(6) establishes the corresponding retention obligation for deployers where those logs are under their control. [2]

These frameworks reflect a clear direction:

- opaque AI systems are increasingly difficult to govern;
- investigations require durable records;
- oversight requires defined human roles;
- consequential activity must remain traceable;
- evidence must survive beyond the immediate execution context.

But a deeper question sits beneath traceability:

After models, prompts, workflows, policies, and personnel have changed, can anyone still reconstruct what the AI received, what it produced, what humans reviewed, what decision followed, and what the evidence actually established at that time?

That is not merely a question of whether logs exist. It is a question of what those logs preserve—and what they leave ambiguous.

## Why Activity Logs Are Not Enough

Most organizations already generate extensive technical records.

Applications emit trace identifiers, timestamps, metrics, and error states. Pipelines may store inputs and outputs. Dashboards can show that a model endpoint was called and that a response was returned at a particular time.

Those records are essential for system health and operational diagnosis. They are not always sufficient for institutional accountability.

### Authority structure

A record may contain:

```text
risk_score = 0.94
```

But the value alone does not reveal its authority.

Was it:

- asserted by an upstream source;
- calculated by a model;
- assigned by a human reviewer;
- generated by a policy engine;
- accepted as an institutional finding;
- or merely preserved without endorsement?

A value can be accurately logged while its institutional meaning remains unclear.

### Attribution

Workflow records often combine source-system data, AI output, human edits, and institutional decisions into one flattened event stream.

A later reviewer may be unable to determine:

- which actor supplied a statement;
- who altered it;
- whether the AI recommended or decided;
- whether a human reviewed or merely opened the record;
- whether the institution adopted the output or only retained it.

### Policy and configuration context

A timestamp alone may not establish:

- which policy version governed;
- which model or configuration was identified;
- which mapping rules were active;
- which exceptions applied;
- whether the current meaning of a field is the same meaning it carried at event time.

### Mechanical status versus interpretation

A cryptographic digest may verify. A packet may contain every declared member. A mapping operation may execute deterministically.

None of those facts automatically establishes that:

- the source assertion was true;
- the human interpretation was correct;
- the decision complied with policy;
- the evidence was legally sufficient;
- the institutional outcome was justified.

Mechanical verification and institutional judgment are different evidentiary types.

### Unresolved boundaries

Ordinary logs rarely preserve what was deliberately left unresolved.

They may not distinguish:

- `NOT_CHECKED` from `PASS`;
- `NOT_ESTABLISHED` from `FALSE`;
- source assertion from verified fact;
- recorded review from exercised judgment;
- client approval from source truth;
- mechanical integrity from aggregate trust.

Activity history is necessary. But activity history is not automatically an independently reconstructable evidence record.

## Reconstructive Fidelity: A Proposed Definition

In Fork—a bounded evidence architecture being developed for AI-assisted workflows—**reconstructive fidelity** means:

> The degree to which a later reviewer can recover the content, attribution, authority, temporal context, mechanical status, and unresolved boundaries of an earlier institutional event without relying solely on the institution’s current interpretation of it.

This is a proposed evidentiary concept, not a claim that the term already has a settled industry-wide definition.

Several dimensions are contained within it.

### Content

What inputs, outputs, source materials, review records, and decisions were present?

### Attribution

Which elements came from:

- a source system;
- an AI model;
- a human reviewer;
- a policy engine;
- Fork;
- or the institution acting through an authorized decision-maker?

### Authority

Who had authority to define the meaning of a field, interpret the record, determine policy satisfaction, or adopt an operative institutional position?

### Temporal context

When did the event occur, and which models, configurations, mappings, policies, and authorities were operative at that time?

### Mechanical status

What can be independently recomputed or checked from the preserved evidence, including:

- declared packet membership;
- artifact digests;
- manifest digests;
- seal bindings;
- schema conformance;
- declared relationships;
- verification-result integrity?

### Unresolved boundaries

What did the system explicitly refuse to establish about:

- truth;
- completeness;
- legality;
- compliance;
- sufficiency;
- admissibility;
- institutional correctness?

Reconstructive fidelity is not a determination that a decision was good, correct, or lawful.

It is the preservation of enough evidentiary structure that authorized reviewers can examine the decision as it occurred rather than as it is later remembered.

## The Invariant Distance Principle

Reconstructive fidelity requires more than detailed records. It requires earlier states to remain stable enough to compare with later ones.

Fork proposes the **Invariant Distance Principle**:

> The Invariant Distance Principle requires stable evidentiary anchors for each institutional state so that later changes in authority, interpretation, configuration, and time can be compared without being collapsed into the institution’s current account of itself.

Two boundaries are essential.

### The anchor is evidentiary, not truth

An invariant evidentiary anchor may include:

- preserved artifact bytes;
- attributed source;
- effective time;
- authority state;
- declared scope;
- packet membership;
- integrity-verification result.

From those coordinates, a system may legitimately establish:

> This is the same preserved record.

Or:

> This is not the same preserved record.

It cannot, from integrity alone, establish:

> Every claim inside the record was complete, correct, or true.

The governing distinction is:

> **Invariant evidentiary anchor ≠ invariant truth**

Integrity of the record and truth of the recorded claim are different questions.

### Distance is multidimensional

Institutional states may differ across independent coordinates:

- formal text;
- authority source;
- effective scope;
- covered actors;
- rights and obligations;
- exceptions;
- decision procedures;
- implementation;
- reliance;
- observed consequences;
- effective period.

The distance between two states should therefore not be silently compressed into one universal score.

It is better represented initially as a vector:

```text
DISTANCE(A, A′) = {
  textual_change,
  authority_change,
  scope_change,
  procedure_change,
  obligation_change,
  exception_change,
  implementation_change,
  consequence_change
}
```

Some dimensions may be established. Others may remain `NOT_CHECKED` or `NOT_ESTABLISHED`.

Any later weighting of those differences into an aggregate judgment requires its own declared method and authority. Fork does not silently decide which institutional differences matter most.

It preserves the coordinates so an authorized reviewer can decide.

## Institutional Drift and Reformalization

Institutional change does not always proceed in one direction.

A recurring pattern is:

```text
STATE A
→ STATE B
→ CLAIM OF RETURN TO A
→ STATE A′
```

Where:

- **A** is an original doctrine, policy, classification, or operating state;
- **B** is an intervening rupture or replacement;
- the institution later claims to restore or return to A;
- **A′** is the newly authorized state established after B.

It is tempting to describe A′ as simply “A again.”

But this is never historically exact.

The intervening state B occurred. It governed real conduct. It affected people, decisions, reliance, expectations, precedent, institutional memory, and consequences.

The sharper formulation is:

> **A′ is not A restored. It is a new state that claims ancestry from A.**

This phenomenon may be described as **reformalization**: the reauthorization of an earlier formal order after an intervening state, producing not the original state again but a newly constituted state that claims continuity with it.

The restoration claim is itself an attributed institutional act. Fork may preserve:

- who made the claim;
- under what authority;
- when it was made;
- which prior state it referenced;
- what formal configuration was actually adopted.

Fork does not need to adopt the institution’s theory of continuity.

A prominent legal example is *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*. The United States Supreme Court overruled the *Chevron* doctrine and held that courts must exercise independent judgment when interpreting statutes and determining whether agencies acted within their statutory authority. The Court also stated that overruling *Chevron* did not itself call prior holdings relying on that framework into question; those holdings remain subject to statutory stare decisis. [3]

The governing doctrine changed. The legal consequences produced during the intervening period did not simply disappear.

A reformalization-aware evidence model therefore preserves:

- A as it actually operated;
- B as it actually operated;
- the restoration claim as a separately attributed act;
- A′ as actually constituted;
- the observable differences among them.

The invariant is:

> A′ may supersede B going forward, but it may not retroactively make B never have governed.

## Institutional Continuity Without Institutional Amnesia

The same structure applies to ordinary institutional decision-making.

Fork distinguishes among three kinds of state:

### Observed state

What did the contemporaneous evidence support?

### Determined state

What did an authorized actor later conclude?

### Operative state

What position does the institution currently rely upon or act under?

These states may align. Fork does not assume that they do.

For example:

```text
OBSERVED:
review activity was recorded

DETERMINED:
the policy owner later concluded that the review satisfied policy

OPERATIVE:
the institution accepted the case as approved
```

The later determination may legitimately change the operative state.

It cannot change the historical fact that the original evidentiary state was narrower.

The governing rule is:

> **Resolution may change the current institutional state without changing the historical evidentiary state.**

A later interpretation becomes a new attributed artifact. It does not silently rewrite the earlier record.

This permits institutional learning without institutional amnesia.

## Fork as a Bounded Architectural Example

The Invariant Distance Principle emerged through the development of Fork, a system designed to produce recomputable evidence for AI-assisted workflows.

The practical challenge was not simply to preserve more workflow data.

It was to preserve distinctions among:

- what a source asserted;
- what the AI produced;
- what Fork observed;
- what mechanically verified;
- what a human interpreted;
- what the institution decided;
- what remained unresolved.

Fork’s reference architecture and declared test corpus demonstrate:

- explicit, declared packet membership;
- canonical SHA-256 member and manifest digests;
- HMAC-SHA-256 binding of the manifest digest;
- verification from persisted artifacts without reliance on the original construction path;
- construction-path-independent recomputation of membership, digests, and seal binding;
- granular `PASS`, `FAIL`, and `NOT_CHECKED` results;
- absence of aggregate trust, validity, compliance, or admissibility verdicts;
- export of preserved verification results with explicit artifact inventory and digests. [4]

These capabilities remain bounded.

They do not, by themselves, establish:

- source truth or completeness;
- public signer identity or non-repudiation;
- legal admissibility;
- compliance or ethical correctness;
- third-party verifier independence;
- live institutional deployment;
- production readiness.

Fork is therefore not presented as a complete AI-governance platform.

It is a bounded evidentiary architecture designed to preserve enough content, attribution, authority, and mechanical state that AI-assisted workflow evidence can later be independently examined.

## A Six-Layer Evidentiary Model

These layers correspond directly to the deficiencies identified in ordinary activity records. Content preserves what was present. Attribution preserves who supplied or changed it. Mechanical status separates recomputable checks from judgment. The inference boundary prevents integrity from becoming truth. Resolution authority identifies who may legitimately address what remains unresolved, and resolution history preserves later determinations without rewriting earlier states.

Fork’s broader doctrine can be expressed through six layers:

1. **Content**  
   What value, statement, artifact, or event was present?

2. **Attribution**  
   Who supplied, observed, interpreted, approved, or decided it?

3. **Mechanical status**  
   What can Fork independently recompute or verify?

4. **Inference boundary**  
   What conclusions remain unauthorized, unsupported, or unresolved?

5. **Resolution authority**  
   Who, if anyone, is authorized to address what Fork leaves unresolved?

6. **Resolution history**  
   How was a later determination added without rewriting the earlier state?

This model reflects a constitutional pattern:

- Preserve what was recorded.
- Attribute who asserted it.
- Verify only what can be recomputed.
- Retain what remains unresolved.
- Identify who may legitimately resolve it.
- Preserve any resolution without rewriting the earlier record.

The goal is not to create a universal ontology for every institution.

The goal is to preserve locally authorized meanings while maintaining globally stable evidentiary boundaries.

An institution may define what `approved` means in its workflow.

Fork can preserve:

- that the value `approved` was recorded;
- who defined the term;
- which source supplied it;
- when it was operative;
- what Fork mechanically verified.

Fork does not silently infer:

- policy satisfaction;
- correctness;
- legality;
- finality;
- equivalence to approval in another institution.

## The Earned Invariant and Its Limits

Within Fork’s governed evidence boundary, a strong—but carefully scoped—engineering statement becomes possible.

Given:

- a fixed declared packet boundary;
- an independently retained reference anchor;
- a declared verification method;
- uncompromised verification material;

a preserved artifact cannot be mutated or substituted and still be represented as unchanged without producing a detectable inconsistency.

Under those conditions:

- different bytes cannot verify as the original sealed artifact;
- a later state cannot silently occupy an earlier state’s evidentiary identity;
- a new interpretation cannot become a prior fact without either mutation or a new attributed record.

This is a claim about evidentiary identity, not universal truth.

Fork cannot prevent:

- an institution from telling a different story;
- a person from ignoring the evidence;
- a source from withholding material;
- an actor from refusing to preserve an event;
- a fully compromised trust domain from regenerating a locally coherent false package.

That is why independently retained anchors, external timestamping, foreign verification, and separately controlled reference material matter.

The narrower invariant is:

> **Within the declared method and fixed trust anchors, the anchor and the drift cannot be silently collapsed without leaving detectable evidence of the transition.**

Fork does not require an actor to confess that a preserved record changed.

Given the declared method and intact anchor, the changed record identifies itself as different.

## The Governance Consequence

NIST and the EU AI Act reflect an emerging governance baseline: consequential AI systems require risk management, documentation, logging, monitoring, retention, oversight, and accountability. [1][2]

Those requirements are necessary.

They do not, by themselves, guarantee reconstructive fidelity.

As AI-assisted activity scales, institutions will need evidence infrastructure capable of preserving:

- what occurred;
- who asserted what;
- what authority each actor possessed;
- what mechanically verified;
- what remained unchecked or unresolved;
- what the institution later determined;
- how the operative state changed;
- what earlier states must remain visible.

Fork is one attempt to supply that evidentiary layer for AI-assisted workflows.

> **Fork provides recomputable evidence for AI-assisted workflows: preserving the anchor, exposing the distance, and keeping later interpretation from silently becoming earlier fact.**

Equally important is what Fork does not do:

> **Fork does not determine whether the recorded decision was true, lawful, compliant, or correct. It preserves the evidence and authority structure required for those determinations to be made without rewriting what occurred.**

AI systems will continue to change.

Institutional interpretations will continue to shift.

Policies and doctrines will be replaced, revised, and sometimes “restored.”

The record of what actually occurred must remain stable enough that those changes can be examined without allowing the institution’s current position to consume its history.

The Invariant Distance Principle provides one way to state that requirement.

Reconstructive fidelity provides one way to evaluate progress toward it.

Fork offers one bounded architectural example of how those ideas can be made executable in code for AI-assisted workflow evidence, with real institutional application remaining the next validation threshold.

The thesis is not that one system solves AI governance.

It is that AI governance cannot succeed without evidence capable of surviving institutional change.
