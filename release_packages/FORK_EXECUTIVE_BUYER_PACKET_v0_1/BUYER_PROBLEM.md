# Buyer Problem

## The short version

Organizations are adopting AI faster than they are preserving the evidence needed to reconstruct AI-assisted work.

That creates exposure.

The issue is not only whether AI systems are governed.

The issue is whether the organization can later show what happened.

## Why current systems are insufficient

Many existing systems preserve partial fragments:

- model logs
- workflow tickets
- approval records
- screenshots
- policy documents
- GRC records
- audit notes
- vendor dashboards
- email trails
- document histories

But those fragments often remain distributed, mutable, incomplete, or hard to interpret later.

They may not preserve the hand-off between:

- AI assistance
- human review
- authority
- approval
- execution
- audit
- compliance
- risk
- security
- remediation
- reporting

Fork is designed around that hand-off problem.

## What buyers fear

Buyers are not primarily afraid of another abstract AI ethics failure.

They are afraid of being unable to answer:

- What did the AI system contribute?
- What did the human reviewer see?
- What evidence was available?
- What policy applied at the time?
- Who approved or denied the action?
- What actually occurred?
- Was the record changed?
- What was not checked?
- What was not captured?
- What cannot be responsibly claimed?

## Why this matters

The inability to reconstruct a consequential AI-assisted workflow can create:

- legal exposure
- compliance risk
- audit failure
- vendor governance risk
- operational uncertainty
- security investigation gaps
- remediation delays
- reporting uncertainty
- loss of internal accountability

Fork does not eliminate those risks.

Fork helps preserve the evidence basis needed to evaluate them.
