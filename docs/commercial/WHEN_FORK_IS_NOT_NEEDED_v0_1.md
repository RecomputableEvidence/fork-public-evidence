# When Fork Is Not Needed v0.1

Fork is designed for AI-assisted workflows where later reconstruction of reliance matters.

It is not necessary for every AI interaction.

---

## Fork may not be needed when:

### 1. The output is purely ephemeral

If an AI output is used only for brainstorming, drafting, exploration, or temporary personal productivity, and does not become part of an institutional decision, Fork may add unnecessary overhead.

### 2. There is no downstream reliance

If no person, team, system, reviewer, auditor, customer, regulator, or decision process will later rely on the artifact, a recomputable reliance record may not be needed.

### 3. The workflow is already fully deterministic and self-contained

If the process is deterministic, internally logged, and does not involve AI-assisted interpretation, summarization, recommendation, or claim movement, Fork may not provide meaningful additional value.

### 4. Existing records already preserve the necessary boundary context

If an organization can already show what was requested, what was produced, what evidence was referenced, what was reviewed, what claims were preserved, what was excluded, and whether the record remains verifiable, Fork may be redundant.

### 5. The organization only needs runtime control

If the primary need is prompt filtering, model access control, policy enforcement, guardrails, or model monitoring, Fork is not the right primary tool.

Fork is designed to complement those systems, not replace them.

### 6. No audit, legal, compliance, risk, or review question is expected

If later reconstruction would not matter operationally, legally, commercially, or institutionally, Fork is likely unnecessary.

---

## Fork is most useful when:

An AI-assisted artifact moves from generation into institutional action, and a later reviewer may need to ask:

**What was actually relied upon, what was not, and can the preserved record still be verified?**

Fork should be used where the cost of ambiguity is higher than the cost of preserving the evidence boundary.
